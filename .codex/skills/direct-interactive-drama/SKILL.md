# Direct Interactive Drama

Direct what the player understands, asks, does, and feels over time. Do not assume prior knowledge of the source.

## Build the performance plan

1. Read the production charter, approved story model, approved gameplay design, and [onboarding-and-pacing.md](references/onboarding-and-pacing.md).
2. Define the player's entry knowledge, false assumptions, immediate need, control knowledge, and first unanswered question.
3. Design onboarding through playable action. Teach only the verb needed now and remove scaffolding after demonstrated use.
4. Divide the dramatic spine into beats. Give every beat an intention, player question, objective, obstacle, actor tactic, required action, reveal, turn, consequence, intensity, cooldown, and exit gate.
5. Alternate staged performance, bounded exploration, conversation, consequence, and transition. Do not interrupt for confirmation after every line.
6. Create an intensity curve with deliberate build, peak, fade, and rest. Give major reveals time to land before the next required task.
7. Author dialogue intent and representative lines. Use action, subtext, gaze, silence, props, sound, and space before explanatory narration.
8. Write `generated/30-performance/performance-plan.json` following [performance-contract.md](references/performance-contract.md).

## Guard comprehension and agency

- Never reveal information unavailable to the player role or speaker.
- Never advance solely on elapsed time when comprehension or action is required.
- Require an observable demonstration before considering a taught mechanic understood.
- Provide optional reminders and an on-demand objective; do not repeat all exposition.
- Ensure every scene changes knowledge, position, possession, relationship, danger, access, or intent.
- End each beat on an emotional or causal turn, not merely the last source line.

Do not choose map coordinates, generate final art, or change approved mechanics.
